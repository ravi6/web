The purpose of this note is to communicate analytical results estimating the extent of changes in the concentration of an unspecified contaminant within the product stream due to adsorption/desorption cycles in the OBOGS bed. The objective here is not to examine a specific contaminant, but to gauge the relative changes in contaminant concentration between the inlet and product streams across different throughput levels. This is evaluated under the crude assumption that the contaminant follows the adsorption and desorption characteristics of N2.

The performance curve for the OBOGS presented above is derived from experimental data obtained at a 50 psig supply pressure. The waste stream O2 level is calculated using an assumed rejection factor, defined as the ratio of product air flow to the inlet air flow rate of the OBOGS unit. This rejection ratio depends closely on the operating conditions of the OBOGS system (such as operating pressure, as well as cycle times for pressurisation, depressurisation, and pressure equalisation). Strictly speaking, the rejection factor can vary with throughput. However, for this analysis, it is assumed to be constant across the range of considered product flows because measured data for the waste flow rate was not readily available.

Assumption: The contaminant is adsorbed by the bed to the exact same degree as nitrogen. With this framework, we dictate that the fraction of the contaminant stream appearing in the product stream relative to the inlet stream matches the magnitude of the fraction of N2 appearing in the product stream relative to the inlet stream. A similar matching assumption is applied to the waste stream calculations.

According to this analysis, as the product flow rate is increased, the contaminant concentration increases alongside it, mirroring the upward trend seen in N2 concentration. Consequently, any operational strategy aimed at reducing the O2 level in the product stream by scaling up throughput will invariably increase the concentration of the contaminant. For instance, if one considers reducing the concentration of the O2 level at a breathing rate of 20 lpm (with a corresponding 96% O2 purity) down to 60% by doubling the product flow to 40 lpm and discarding the additional 20 lpm, a roughly nine-fold increase in product contaminant concentration occurs (jumping from 5 ppm to 45 ppm).

Conversely, if one assumes that the contaminant behaves similarly to Oxygen adsorption dynamics, it can be observed that the contaminant concentration in the product stream peaks at low demand levels (reaching roughly 4.5 times the input concentration) and gradually declines as the output flow rate scales upward.

In essence, completely divergent behaviors regarding contaminant concentration dynamics can be anticipated depending entirely on its underlying adsorption profile. Furthermore, if a contaminant is irreversibly adsorbed to a certain extent, the overall concentration profiles do not alter their shape, though the absolute concentration magnitudes across both streams attenuate.

Finally, it must be stressed that real-world contaminant concentrations and adsorption profiles vary widely. The intent of this analysis is not to accurately predict absolute concentrations, but rather to glean overall behavioral trends based on strong or weak adsorption propensities relative to primary air constituents.
